COVID-19 Fact Checker

Let's face it, there is an overwhelming amount of information out there about COVID-19 and much of it is false. Below are a number of statements about COVID-19, some true and some false. Read each statement and click on the accompanying button to reveal the facts.

1. The coronavirus responsible for COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2) was created in a Chinese lab.

FALSE. This claim has been made by many, including the President of the United States. To be absolutely clear here, there is NO evidence to support this claim. Virologists who spend their careers hunting the origin of viruses (and not your unhinged uncle who constantly forwards you conspiracy theories he learned from his Facebook friends) have concluded that the most likely origin of SARS-CoV-2 is bats. If you're contributing to the spread of misinformation about the origins of the virus responsible for COVID-19, stop.

2. The travel ban from China to the US was early and saved millions of lives.

FALSE. By the time the US imposed travel restrictions from China on Feb 2, forty-five nations had already done so. And after this date, tens of thousands continued to arrive from China to the US. Furthermore, it was confirmed that person-to-person transmission was occurring before the travel restrictions were imposed. And it has been established that the origin of virus in the largest early outbreak, New York City, was Europe. Given these facts, it seems doubtful the travel restrictions from China to the US had a significant impact on the course of the pandemic in the US.

3. COVID-19 is far more deadly than the flu.

TRUE. Based upon CDC estimates from the 2018-2019 flu season the mortality rate from the seasonal flu is estimated to be around 0.1% (meaning approximately 1 in a 1000 who contract the flu will die). Calculating the mortality rate from a novel virus in the midst of a pandemic is somewhat of a challenge, but a number of studies have estimated a rate of 0.5-1.0% (suggesting a mortality rate of 5 to 10 times that of the seasonal flu). Aside from the relatively high mortality rates associated with COVID-19, an unknown number of survivors appear to suffer from symptoms following the acute phase of infection for an extended period of time.

4. We now have effective treatments for COVID-19.

TRUE, with caveats. Despite numerous claims that hydroxychloroquine is an effective treatment for COVID-19 (including claims made by the President of the United States), the NIH COVID-19 Treatment Guidelines Panel recommends against the use of hydroxychloroquine for the treatment of COVID-19 due to a lack of demonstrated benefit and the potential for harm. Although it does not appear to reduce the death rate, the antiviral medication remdesivir appears to speed the recovery of those hospitalized with COVID-19. In contrast to remdesivir, the steroid dexamethasone has been proven to reduce the death rate among those hospitalized with COVID-19 who require mechanical ventilation or supplemental oxygen. In the absence of published data, it is difficult to assess the efficacy of monoclonal antibody "cocktails" in the treatment of COVID-19. Additionally, with over 50,000 Americans being diagnosed with COVID-19 daily, it seems likely the demand for antibody treatments would far outstrip the available supply. For the majority of those diagnosed with COVID-19, as with the flu, the most effective remedy currently available is time. For those ill enough to require hospitalization remdesivir may speed recovery and dexamethasone may reduce the odds of dying. The potentially promising therapy of monoclonal antibodies will likely be limited in supply for the foreseeable future.

5. Masks help prevent the spread of COVID-19.

TRUE. The most common mode of transmission for COVID-19 is via respiratory droplets. When people with COVID-19 cough, sneeze, sing, speak, or breathe, they produce respiratory droplets. These droplets can then be inhaled or deposited on the mucus membranes of those within close contact of someone with COVID-19. Even a cloth mask can significantly reduce the number of respiratory droplets expelled when speaking. There appears to be some variation in the efficacy of different facial coverings with neck gaiters and bandanas being the least effective. Need more empirical evidence? Countries with cultural norms or government policies favoring mask use appear to have lower death rates from COVID-19. Within the US, 2 hairstylists with confirmed COVID-19 who wore cloth masks cut the hair of 140 clients (who wore a mix of different mask types) over an 8 day period; no new infections appeared to have resulted from either stylist.

6. I don't currently have any symptoms and therefore cannot spread COVID-19, right?

FALSE. Laboratory data suggest that people who have contracted COVID-19 appear to be most infectious 2 days before developing symptoms. Additionally, it is currently estimated that 40% of those with COVID-19 may never develop symptoms and yet can still transmit the disease. The reality is that anyone* can transmit COVID-19 without being aware they were infected. Ideally, we'd all behave accordingly.

*There is documented evidence that reinfection can occur. Individuals previously infected with COVID-19 should continue to take the same precautions as others.

7. I only have a mild cough, runny nose, and no fever, this can't be COVID-19, right?

FALSE. People with COVID-19 have had a wide range of symptoms reported from mild symptoms to severe illness. While a fever and a persistent cough are the most common symptoms associated with COVID-19, researchers have found that more than 1 in 5 with COVID-19 have no fever. Symptoms associated with COVID-19 include fever, cough, shortness of breath, loss of taste and smell, muscle aches, headache, fatigue, runny nose, sore throat, vomiting, and diarrhea. The reality is that without testing it can be impossible in many cases to exclude COVID-19 by symptoms alone.

8. "We're rounding the turn. We're rounding the corner. It's going away." - October 22, 2020

FALSE. The day after this statement was made, the US reported a record high 83 thousand new COVID-19 cases. Additionally, on Oct 22, the 7-day average number of daily deaths from COVID-19 in the US was 789 and rising. As October 2020 draws to a close, by no objective measure is the US "rounding the corner" on COVID-19.

9. I came in close contact with someone with COVID-19. If I test negative I don't need to quarantine, right?

FALSE. While it is reasonable to consider testing following a high risk exposure to a known COVID-19 case, completing a 14 day quarantine from others is recommended regardless of a test result. It is well established that symptoms may develop 2-14 days following exposure. A negative test does NOT exclude the possibility one may develop symptoms later and spread the disease.
